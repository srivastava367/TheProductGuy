Product Thinking

If I Were the PM of
This Email System

On the quiet failure of systems that optimize for sending, not for being welcomed.

I have been receiving emails from the same sender since 2025. I rarely open them. They keep coming anyway.

Six emails. Ten days. Same sender. Same pitch. None opened.

Look at those subject lines. "Re: Santosh." "Re: Interview." "Re: Sandeep." The "Re:" prefix implies a reply to a conversation that never happened. It is a design decision someone made deliberately, which makes it worse than accidental spam. At some point this stopped feeling like marketing and started feeling like a system that has genuinely no model of me as a person. It knows my name. It does not know anything else.

The frustrating part is that this is not a hard problem. It is a prioritization problem. Someone decided that send volume mattered more than signal quality, and that decision is baked into every email I ignore.

So here is what I would fix if I owned it.

1 Signal Processing

Treat non-opens as data, not as permission to try harder

No opens across three months is not a streak of bad luck. It is a user telling you something clearly without using words. Most email systems respond to this by increasing frequency or changing the subject line. Both are wrong answers to the right signal.

A well-designed system would reduce cadence after five consecutive non-opens, shift to a different channel or format after ten, and pause entirely after fifteen. Not forever. Just long enough to stop being noise.

Persistence without feedback is not resilience. It is a system that has stopped listening.

2 Honesty in Design

Fake personalization is worse than no personalization

"Re: Santosh" in the subject line is not a conversation. It is a trick that worked once in 2017 and has since trained an entire generation of users to be suspicious of their own names in inboxes.

Real personalization is behavioral, not cosmetic. It means showing someone who browsed visa requirements a different email than someone who clicked on salary data. Same product. Completely different entry points. That takes more work but it is the only kind that actually earns attention.

Users do not want to feel known. They want to feel understood. Those are not the same thing.

3 Narrative Architecture

Stop repeating yourself. Build a journey instead.

Every email in this sequence says the same thing in a slightly different way: get a job abroad. After the third one, the user has heard the pitch. Sending it again is not reminding them. It is assuming they forgot, which is both inaccurate and slightly insulting.

A sequence should have a shape. Each email should assume the previous one was read and move the conversation forward.

Email 1 The problem you might not have named yet
Email 2 Proof that others have moved through it
Email 3 Exactly how the process works, without softening it
Email 4 A single, specific action with a low barrier

Same destination. A path that actually gets there.

4 Cooldown Logic

Build the pause into the system, not as an afterthought

If someone ignores ten emails, the eleventh email is not a fresh start. It is the eleventh ignored email. The user is not waiting for a better subject line. They have moved on or are not ready yet, and both of those states require the same response: stop.

A 45-day pause resets the relationship. When the next email arrives after genuine silence, it has the quality of an unexpected return rather than an unrelenting presence. Scarcity is not manipulation. It is respect for someone's attention.

5 The Real Metric

Optimize for being welcomed, not for being seen

Open rates go up when you send more email. Unsubscribe rates also go up. Trust goes down quietly, with no metric attached, and you do not notice until the list has rotted.

The question worth asking is not "how do we get more opens this week?" It is "what would make someone glad this arrived?" Those two questions lead to completely different systems.

Growth without trust is just churn that has not shown up in the dashboard yet.

None of this is technically difficult. Email platforms support all of it. The problem is not capability. It is the incentive structure that rewards volume over relationship, sends over signal, and short-term opens over long-term welcome.

The job of a well-designed communication system is not to be seen as many times as possible. It is to be the thing someone is actually glad opened in their inbox.

Any system that cannot tell when to stop has not been designed. It has just been pointed in a direction and left running.
