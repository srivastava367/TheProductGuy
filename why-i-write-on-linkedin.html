On writing & thinking

Why I Write
on LinkedIn

Not for reach. Not for the algorithm. But because writing is the only tool I have found that turns noise into something I can actually think with.

Santosh Srivastava 6 min read Product · Writing · Process

The Honest Answer

It is not about visibility.
It never was.

Most people assume LinkedIn writing is a transaction you post to be seen, to build a brand, to stay top of mind with recruiters. There is nothing wrong with that logic. But it is not why I write. I write because I genuinely enjoy it. And more practically, I write because it is the only process I have found that forces me to slow down long enough to make sense of what is otherwise just noise.

The world does not slow down for you. Products ship, markets shift, conversations blur into one another. You consume far more than you can process. And somewhere in that accumulation, your actual thinking the kind that matters gets lost. Writing does not solve this problem entirely. But it creates a friction that is useful. It demands that you stop, sit with an idea, and find out whether you actually understand it or just recognise it.

That distinction between recognition and understanding is the whole reason I show up on a blank page. Recognition is fast and shallow. Understanding is slow and honest. Writing, at its best, is the act of converting one into the other.

The blank page does not care about your reputation. It only asks: do you actually know what you think you know?

Personal observation

LinkedIn is simply the surface I chose. Not because it is the best medium for long-form thought it is not but because the people I want to think alongside are already here. Product managers, founders, operators, curious generalists. The platform is imperfect and loud, but the audience is real. And writing in public, however imperfectly, creates a kind of accountability that writing in a private notebook does not.

Raw Material

What I actually write about

I write about what my mind goes through what my eyes notice, what my ears pick up. Things I read. Things I feel. Small observations that most of us experience but rarely pause to articulate.

This is a broader brief than it sounds. A long road trip that reveals something true about how people make decisions. A product feature I keep misusing that tells me something about the design brief that was never written down. A conversation with a colleague that surfaces an assumption everyone held silently for months. A sentence in a book that reframes something I thought I already understood.

None of these are inherently LinkedIn content. That is precisely why they become interesting when you try to write them down. The exercise of fitting an observation into language of finding the argument inside the anecdote is where the actual thinking happens. The platform is almost incidental. What matters is the practice of translation: experience into insight, observation into language, noise into signal.

01

Things you read

Not summaries. Not hot takes. But the second-order question buried inside the idea what does this actually imply if you follow it through?

02

Things you feel

Discomfort is data. When something in a product, a meeting, or a market move bothers you, that reaction is worth investigating before it fades.

03

Small observations

The ideas that go unspoken in every team, every industry, every conversation. Articulating the obvious is underrated. It is rarely as obvious as you think.

04

Lived experience

A road trip. A migration project. A failed experiment. The distance between experience and lesson is a sentence waiting to be written.

The Process

The loop that happens
before you hit post

Everyone has a creative process. Most people just have not named it. Mine is a loop and it matters that it is a loop, not a pipeline. You do not move linearly from idea to publication. You spiral. You return. You question what you thought you had settled, and you settle what you thought you were still questioning.

Here is what that looks like in practice:

The Writing Loop

  1. 01

    Capture raw thoughts as they come

    No structure, no pressure. Just notes. A voice memo, a half-sentence in a notes app, a margin annotation. The idea in its most unguarded form.

  2. 02

    Expand the thoughts into a narrative

    Find the through-line. What is this actually about? What am I trying to say, and does the shape of the piece match the weight of the idea?

    Still not posting
  3. 03

    Bring in an LLM to refine structure and flow

    I use AI as a thoughtful editor to sharpen structure, surface gaps in logic, improve clarity. But the instruction is always the same: the voice should still sound like me.

    Still not posting
  4. 04

    Read it again. Question it. Trim it.

    The final read is the most important one. Does it still say what I meant to say? Is the original intent intact? Sometimes whole sections get rewritten here.

    Still not posting
  5. 05

    Post. Only then.

    When the piece sounds like me and says what I actually think. Not when the window of relevance demands it. Not when the draft is good enough. When it is honest.

The AI step in that loop is worth dwelling on. A lot of writers treat LLMs as ghostwriters or shortcuts. I do not. I treat them as a particularly patient first reader one that will tell me where the argument gets foggy and where the structure collapses without being polite about it. But the ideas have to arrive from somewhere real. You cannot outsource that part. The raw thought has to be yours before the refinement means anything.

✕  ✕  ✕

The danger in using AI for writing is not that it writes for you. The danger is that it writes convincingly in a voice that is not quite yours, and you stop noticing the gap. I stay careful about that. Every round of AI editing gets followed by a personal re-read with one question in mind: does this still sound like me thinking, or does it sound like someone trying to sound like me thinking? The gap between those two is larger than most people realise.

The Deeper Point

Everyone has a loop.
Most just don't realise it.

Here is what I have come to believe: the people who seem to think clearly in public are not smarter than the rest of us. They have simply made their thinking process legible: to themselves first, then to others. The loop is not a productivity hack. It is a clarity practice.

When you write regularly, you start to notice patterns in your own thinking. The topics you keep returning to. The assumptions you hold that you have never examined. The arguments you make confidently that fall apart the moment you commit them to a sentence. The writing is not the output it is the diagnostic.

I also think there is something worth saying about the decision not to post. Most people think about writing as a decision to publish. I think about it as a decision to think. Posting is what happens at the end when the thinking has gone somewhere real. The platform is downstream. The process is upstream. Get the process right and the posting takes care of itself.

Writing is not the output. It is the diagnostic. The thing you make when you are trying to find out what you actually think.

Personal observation

None of this is a prescription. Some people think well in conversations. Others think well in numbers, in drawings, in models. The medium matters less than the practice. What I am arguing for is deliberateness knowing your loop and protecting it, rather than letting the algorithm's urgency collapse it into reflex posting.

The most useful thing LinkedIn ever did for me was give me a reason to take my own thinking seriously enough to write it down. The posts are evidence of a process. The process is the actual point.

An open question

Curious how others think before they hit post

Everyone has a loop. A ritual, a filter, a gut check. Most people have never articulated it even to themselves.

What does your pre-post process actually look like? Not the polished version. The real one. Where does the idea begin, and how many times does it almost die before it makes it out?
